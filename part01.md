The data that I chose to collect for this project was my year of activity on Instagram. I didn’t run into any problem collecting the data, and had it transferred as JSON files to my Google Drive. I think the most profound reaction I had to this data was the sheer volume of it. I chose Instagram because I figured that this was the one platform I used the least out of the options listed. I wasn’t shocked at how thorough the data was, but the amount of it made me realize how perfunctory my engagement with social media is.

Going through the data, I realized that I was very consistent in a way I hadn't consciously acknowledged. My feed was almost entirely dominated by architecture-related reels, specifically content that mirrored my interests during undergrad. Looking at the timestamps of my likes, I could see a clear reflection of my academic environment. It was strange to see a year of my life distilled into a series of metadata points, especially considering I perceive my usage as minimal.

I found the whole data a little surreal and almost archival, which I find kind of beautiful(?). My Instagram data and algorithm are not a reflection of who I am currently, but rather a version of me that bifurcated from my past interests. Although the depth of the data makes me slightly uncomfortable, I don’t think the content itself is inherently malevolent. There’s also a sense of reassurance that my social media data doesn’t keep up with my identity, and it feels like I the agency.
